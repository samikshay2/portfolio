The HydroSolar Car

Project: Solar-Hydrogen Vehicle

Course: Principles of Engineering (POE)

Duration: Approximately 2.5 weeks (November 1 - November 16, 2018)

Objective: Design, create, and test a prototype of a vehicle using VEX Robotics parts that uses a solar module and/or fuel cell as its power source.

Type of Project: 4 Person Group Project

Link to Project Documentation: Solar-Hydrogen Vehicle Project Documentation

Images of Project: Double solar cell configuration (left, top) & singe hydrogen cell configuration (left, bottom). Click on the image to zoom in.


The goal of the Solar-Hydrogen Vehicle Project was to design, create, and test a prototype of a vehicle that uses a solar module and/or fuel cell as its sole power source. This prototype would then be used used to gather information about different energy source combinations to formulate a recommendation for optimum power generation. For this project, there were four total power source configurations needed, one with a single solar cell, one with a single hydrogen cell, one with two solar cells, and one with two hydrogen cells. However, there were constraints for this project, including the dimensions (the top view of the vehicle could be no larger than 5 inches by 12 inches), only VEX Robotics parts could be used to build the vehicle, the vehicle should have been able to hold the solar module or hydrogen fuel cell securely and run without tipping the fuel cell, it must use a breadboard to easily change between power source configurations, and there was a time limit of 16 days. For this project, I wired the four configurations of the breadboard, including making a parallel and series circuit for the double cell configurations. In addition, I revised our initial design sketch to optimize the torque of the vehicle over speed and make the vehicle more efficient by reducing the parts of the vehicle that increased weight and friction. Charging the hydrogen cells, recording measurements, and doing the calculations for work, input electrical power, output mechanical power, and efficiency were also among my tasks. Lastly, I was the project manager for this project and planned the timeline of this project using a Gantt chart. Some skills I learned through this project were prototyping a vehicle, breadboard configurations, multimeter usage, and project management skills (Gantt chart and decision matrices). We created a Project Design Notebook that is linked under the section for project documentation. I learned various skills, including programming samples for computer science (MIT App Inventor Environment), app developmentin particular.

The Rockettes

Project: 2.1.4 Aerodynamic Design Project

Course: Principles of Engineering (POE)

Duration: Approximately 3 weeks (January 8 - January 28, 2019)

Objective: To design six total projectiles as a team, maximizing horizontal displacement.

Type of Project: 3 Person Group Project

Link to Project Documentation: Aerodynamic Design Documentation


Images of Project: Projectile 1 (left, bottom).


For this project, the goal for each designer was to design, build, and test two projectiles, one to be launched on each of the two launch days. The objective was to make a projectile travel the farthest lateral distance while satisfying the constraints. These prototypes would then be used used to gather information about which designs and launch angles would produce projectiles that would fly the furthest. For this project, there were six total projectiles needed, with each group member designing two unique projectiles. However, there were some constraints limiting the materials we could use and the variables we could influence, including that the entire projectile had to be paper and tape-based only, the body diameter must fit over a ¾ inch PVC pipe and have a length between 10”-15”, the wingspan must be less than 24”, and the projectile is to be launched at specified psi with provided rocket launcher. We, as the designers could choose the angle of launch, which could be in the range 0 degrees < angle < 90 degrees. When it came to designing my two projectiles, I modeled my two designs after most traditional rockets, featuring a cylindrical body, three to four wings spaced equidistant from each other, a nose cone with a pointy apex, and a pressure cap. Made completely out of cardstock, my prototypes were aimed at being as sturdy as possible while launched straight up into the air. However, the cardstock was significantly heavier than printer paper, and to offset the weight, packing tape was used throughout the design as the adhesive to attach all the parts of the rocket. The packing tape was a strong adhesive, more durable compared to the everyday use one-inch tape, but was thinner and lighter than the duct tape. As for the design of the rocket’s four main components (the body, cone, pressure cap, and wings), similar approaches were taken for each of the four. My first rocket, launched at 60 PSI and 45°, traveled 65 yards (195 feet) laterally in 3.10 seconds. My second rocket, launched at 60 PSI and 45°, traveled 53 yards (159 feet) laterally in 3.61 seconds. Upon analyzing the results of the launches, I learned that skinnier wings and nose cones minimize drag, thus maximizing lateral distance. I also found the ideal launch range to be between 30 and 40 degrees when comparing my two projectiles with my teammates' projectiles. Lastly, I was the project manager for this project and planned the timeline of this project using a Gantt chart. Some skills I learned through this project were designing projectiles, using a projectile launcher, and calculating initial, vertical, and horizontal velocities and calculating vertical and horizontal displacement

Habla Espanol!

Project: Greenfoot Educational Game Remix

Course: AP Computer Science Applications (AP CSA)

Duration: 10 days (September 10 - September 20, 2019)

Objective: To create an educational game implementing at least 5 AP CSA Java concepts.

Type of Project: 3 Person Group Project

Link to Project Documentation: Educational Game Design Documentation

Link to GitHub Repository: Educational Game Documentation


Images of Project: Screenshot of code and final game console


For our project, we modified game to create an interactive, feed-back driven Spanish language learning game. The aim of our game is to both successfully reach 400 points and answer the follow-up Spanish questions correctly. The player begins with 0 points and earns 90 points upon moving the cart to successfully catch the lemons. If the player moves the cart and it touches a bomb, they lose 90 points. The player must be aware however, that the bombs and lemons pop up in random locations and fall at varying speeds. If the player successfully reaches 400 points, he or she will get to choose between 4 different Spanish topics including colors, numbers, verbs, and vocabulary words. Upon deciding which area they would like to review, the player will get asked a series of 3 questions and feedback to these questions will be printed to the console. If all questions are answered correctly, the game will end. Conversely, if the player reaches negative points, as a penalty they must answer the three questions as well. If the player is a stellar student and would like to review a second topic the game also gives them that opportunity. The summer homework topics we used were Boolean operators and logic, Java library classes such as Math, nested if-else statements, string functions and operations, and static variables and methods (static final int). We used string functions mainly to create a question and answer interaction where we first ask the user what topic of spanish they want to practice. The topics we ask for them to choose from are: colors, numbers, vocabulary, or verbs (all in Spanish). After they pick a topic, we ask 3 questions related to it. The if - else statements were used inside the string functions to ask a series of questions and based on user input, providing a different response based on user’s selection. The Math.random method was used to create random behavior and create a more challenging, unpredictable behavior, such as slowing down the speed of the lemons as they fall. This way the user has a higher chance of catching the bomb and having to answer the questions. The boolean functions were used mainly to implement a set of code that runs once the cart touches the lemons or the bombs. Finally, the static int constant variable was implemented to prevent any erratic behavior in movement when the user moves the cart left and right. Overall, I learned several AP CSA Java concepts through this project.

Healthy Heart Helper

Project: Android Studio Skeleton App Project

Course: AP Computer Science Applications (AP CSA)

Duration: Approximately 1 week (September 23 - September 30, 2019)

Objective: To develop a skeleton of an app in Android Studio

Type of Project: 3 Person Group Project

Link to Project Documentation: Android Studio Project Design Documentation

Link to GitHub Repository: Android Studio Project GitHub Documentation

Video demo of the app: Video of my app


Images of Project:


The app that I built is called the Healthy Heart Helper. This app is intended to help people stay informed about their health habits and track their daily nutrition, while giving advice on how to stay healthy. My design plan consisted of the login screen and three activity screen without a settings screen. The current state of the project consists of 4 activity screens. The activity screens are a login screen, Settings menu, nutrition advice screen, and exercise tips screen. The login screen is an activity with a simple layout consisting of the username and password to go to the next screen. The settings screen is a simple layout where there are switches for dark mode, vibrate option, and a checkbox for saving preferences. There is also an about section listing the version of android studio the app is using. The following screen is a nutrition advice screen where there’s a checklist of healthy habits the user should follow and gives them a proper nutrition checklist. The final screen is the exercise tips screen where it lists the type of exercises are recommended to the user based on their demographic (age). These screens are connected and able to go from one activity screen to the other by using buttons through the implementation of intents. The challenges we had were in the process of switching from one activity screen to the other because there were many compiling errors. In addition, we had trouble with Android Studio running smoothly because we often ran into errors with the emulator, build grade file, compiling code without errors, and using linear layout without the app crashing. We eventually fixed these problems by invalidating the caches and restarting, deleting the build.gradle file, creating new activities inside the resources file, and merging the code through Android Studio.
